"distinctive feature
In any language it seems that the sounds used will only differ from each other in a small number of ways. If for example a language had 40 phonemes, then in theory each of those 40 could be utterly different from the other 39. However, in practice there will usually be just a small set of important differences: some of the sounds will be vowels and some consonants; some of the consonants will be plosives and affricates, and the rest will be continuants; some of the continuants will be nasal and some not, and so on. These differences are identified by phonologists, and are known as distinctive features.
There is disagreement about how to define the features (e.g. whether they should be labelled according to articulatory characteristics or acoustic ones), and about how many features are needed in order to be able to classify the sounds of all the languages in the world. See the entry for feature.

"Below is an outline of how vowel systems in human languages tend to evolve over time, from small, “unmarked” inventories to large, complex ones, illustrated with key processes and cross‑linguistic patterns.
---
## 1. The “Unmarked” Core: Five‑Vowel System
Many proto‑languages and modern isolates begin with a maximally stable five‑vowel inventory:
| Front | Central | Back |
| :---: | :-----: | :--: |
| i | | u |
| e | a | o |
* **Examples:** Proto‑Indo‑European, Classical Arabic, Swahili, Standard Spanish
* **Why stable?**
* **Perceptual spacing:** Maximizes acoustic distinctiveness
* **Articulatory ease:** Simple contrasts of high/low and front/back
---
## 2. Expansion: Splits, Length, and Centralization
Starting from five vowels, languages tend to **add** new contrasts via several pathways:
1. **Length distinctions**
* *e* → /e/ vs. /eː/ (e.g. Classical Greek, early Old English)
2. **Diphthongization**
* /eː/ → \[ei̯] → split into new /e/ + /i/ (e.g. Great Vowel Shift in English)
3. **Central vowel emergence**
* Schwa /ə/ appears by reduction of unstressed vowels (e.g. French, many Germanic dialects)
4. **Rounding and fronting/backing splits**
* /u/ → /ʉ/ vs. /u/ (e.g. Norwegian)
* /e/ → /e/ vs. /ø/ (e.g. Old Norse front rounded vowels)
---
## 3. Reduction: Mergers and Losses
Conversely, inventories can **shrink** or simplify by:
* **Mergers**
* Cot–caught (/ɑ/ vs. /ɔ/) merger in North American English dialects
* Pin–pen merger (/ɪ/ vs. /ɛ/) in southern U.S. dialects
* **Neutralization**
* Vowel harmony breakdown: multiple back vowels collapse to a single underspecified /ɐ/ (some dialects of Hindi)
* **Syncope and apocope**
* Loss of medial or final vowels (e.g. Latin → Spanish *pane* → *pan* \[pan])
---
## 4. Chain Shifts
A small change in one vowel often triggers a **chain reaction**:
* **Northern Cities Vowel Shift** (U.S. Great Lakes region)
* /æ/ raises toward \[ɛə], /ɛ/ lowers, /ɪ/ lowers toward \[ɛ], etc.
* **California Vowel Shift**
* Front vowels glide upward; back vowels front.
Chain shifts preserve maximal distinctiveness by moving neighboring vowels in complementary directions.
---
## 5. Areal and Typological Patterns
* **Tonal vs. Non‑tonal correlates:** Tone languages often have fewer vowel qualities but richer tone contrasts (e.g. Mandarin: 5 vowels + 4 tones).
* **Vowel harmony zones:** Uralic, Altaic, and many African families favor harmonic splits (e.g. front vs. back series).
* **Island vs. continental:** Isolated languages (e.g. Pacific islands) often preserve small inventories; contact‑rich areas (e.g. Europe) favor expansions and borrowings.
---
## 6. Phonetic Drivers of Change
* **Articulatory ease:** Tendency to centralize peripheral vowels in unstressed or rapid speech → schwa.
* **Perceptual distinctiveness:** Undershot targets in crowded vowel spaces → chain shifts to maintain contrast.
* **Sociolinguistic factors:** Prestige varieties may introduce shifts (e.g. Received Pronunciation’s trap–bath split).
---
## 7. Historical Case Studies
| Language Family | Proto‑System | Modern Reflexes |
| Indo‑European | *i, e, a, o, u* | English: 20+ vowel phonemes (length, diphthongs, schwa) |
| Bantu | *i, e, a, o, u* | Swahili: retains five; Zulu: adds /ɛ, ɔ/ by vowel harmony |
| Austronesian | *i, u, e, o, a* | Hawaiian: reduces to /i, u, ɑ/; Tagalog: adds schwa |
| Afro‑Asiatic | */i, a, u/* | Arabic: retains three; Hebrew: splits into /i, e, a, o, u/ |
---
## 8. Theoretical Perspectives
* **Feature geometry:** New vowels appear when featural space allows—e.g. \[+round, +front].
* **Optimality Theory:** Rankings shift to favor new contrasts (e.g. *MAX‑ROOT* outranks *DEP‑LONG* → length emerges).
* **Usage‑based models:** Frequency effects cause reduction in high‑frequency vowels → centralization.
---

"Pulmonic Consonants: The Breath-Powered Sounds of Speech
Pulmonic consonants are the foundational sounds of most of the world's languages, produced by the outward flow of air from the lungs. The term "pulmonic" itself is derived from the Latin word pulmo, meaning lung. These consonants are formed when the stream of air, or the airstream, is initiated by the lungs and then obstructed or modified at some point in the vocal tract.
The vast majority of consonants in English and other major world languages are pulmonic. In fact, many languages exclusively use pulmonic consonants for their entire consonant inventory. These sounds are contrasted with non-pulmonic consonants, which utilize different airstream mechanisms, such as the clicks found in some African languages, ejectives, and implosives.
The production of a pulmonic consonant involves a complex interplay of several articulatory features:
Airstream Mechanism: The process begins with the lungs pushing air out, a process known as a pulmonic egressive airstream.
Place of Articulation: This refers to the point in the vocal tract where the airflow is constricted. This can range from the lips (bilabial sounds like /p/ and /b/) to the back of the throat (glottal sounds like /h/). Other places of articulation include the teeth (dental), the alveolar ridge just behind the teeth (alveolar), the hard palate (palatal), and the soft palate or velum (velar).
Manner of Articulation: This describes how the airflow is modified. It can be completely stopped and then released (plosives or stops like /t/ and /d/), forced through a narrow channel (fricatives like /f/ and /s/), or diverted through the nasal cavity (nasals like /m/ and /n/). Other manners include approximants, laterals, and affricates.
Phonation: This refers to the state of the vocal cords during the articulation of the sound. If the vocal cords vibrate, the sound is "voiced" (e.g., /b/, /d/, /g/, /z/). If they do not vibrate, the sound is "voiceless" (e.g., /p/, /t/, /k/, /s/).
The International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA) provides a comprehensive chart of all known pulmonic consonants, systematically organizing them by their place and manner of articulation, as well as their voicing. ConsPhnm.labial

description::
"Labial consonants are consonants in which one or both lips are the active articulator. The two common labial articulations are bilabials, articulated using both lips, and labiodentals, articulated with the lower lip against the upper teeth, both of which are present in English. A third labial articulation is dentolabials, articulated with the upper lip against the lower teeth (the reverse of labiodental), normally only found in pathological speech. Generally precluded are linguolabials, in which the tip of the tongue contacts the posterior side of the upper lip, making them coronals, though sometimes, they behave as labial consonants.[clarification needed]
The most common distribution between bilabials and labiodentals is the English one, in which the nasal and the stops, [m], [p], and [b], are bilabial and the fricatives, [f], and [v], are labiodental. The voiceless bilabial fricative, voiced bilabial fricative, and the bilabial approximant do not exist as the primary realizations of any sounds in English, but they occur in many languages. For example, the Spanish consonant written b or v is pronounced, between vowels, as a voiced bilabial approximant.
Lip rounding, or labialization, is a common approximant-like co-articulatory feature. English /w/ is a voiced labialized velar approximant, which is far more common than the purely labial approximant [β̞]. In the languages of the Caucasus, labialized dorsals like /kʷ/ and /qʷ/ are very common.
Very few languages, however, make a distinction purely between bilabials and labiodentals, making "labial" usually a sufficient specification of a language's phonemes. One exception is Ewe, which has both kinds of fricatives, but the labiodentals are produced with greater articulatory force."

"An apical consonant is a phone (speech sound) produced by obstructing the air passage with the tip of the tongue (apex) in conjunction with upper articulators from lips to postalveolar, and possibly prepalatal.[1][2] It contrasts with laminal consonants, which are produced by creating an obstruction with the blade of the tongue, just behind the tip. ConsPhnm.oral

description::
"An oral consonant is a consonant sound in speech that is made by allowing air to escape from the mouth, as opposed to the nose, as in a nasal consonant. To create an intended oral consonant sound, the entire mouth plays a role in modifying the air's passageway. This rapid modification of the air passageway using the tongue and lips makes changes to the waveform of the sound by compressing and expanding the air. In addition to the nose and mouth, the vocal cords and lungs also make a contribution to producing speech by controlling the volume (amplitude) and pitch (frequency) of the sound. The use of the vocal cords will also determine whether the consonant is voiced or voiceless. The vast majority of consonants are oral, such as, for example [p], [w], [v] and [x]. The others are nasal, such as the nasal occlusives [m] or [ɲ].
Before there appeared the consonantal opposition nasal/oral, consonant was distinguished from vowel as closed tract from open tract. Once the nasal consonant has been opposed to the oral as presence to absence of the open tract, the contrast consonant/vowel is revalued as presence vs. absence of a closed tract.[1]"

ConsPhnm.fricative

description::
"Fricatives are consonants produced by forcing air through a narrow channel made by placing two articulators close together. These may be the lower lip against the upper teeth, in the case of [f]; the back of the tongue against the soft palate, in the case of German [x] (the final consonant of Bach); or the side of the tongue against the molars, in the case of Welsh [ɬ] (appearing twice in the name Llanelli). A particular subset of fricatives are the sibilants. When forming a sibilant, one still is forcing air through a narrow channel, but in addition, the tongue is curled lengthwise to direct the air over the edge of the teeth[1]. English [s], [z], [ʃ], and [ʒ] are examples of sibilants.
The usage of two other terms is less standardized: "Spirant" is an older term for fricatives used by some American and European phoneticians and phonologists. "Strident" could mean just "sibilant", but some authors[who?] include also labiodental and uvular fricatives in the class."

ConsPhnm.sibilant

description::
"Sibilants are fricative consonants of higher amplitude and pitch, made by directing a stream of air with the tongue towards the teeth.[1] Examples of sibilants are the consonants at the beginning of the English words sip, zip, ship, and genre. The symbols in the International Phonetic Alphabet used to denote the sibilant sounds in these words are, respectively, [s] [z] [ʃ] [ʒ]. Sibilants have a characteristically intense sound, which accounts for their paralinguistic use in getting one's attention (e.g. calling someone using "psst!" or quieting someone using "shhhh!").
In the alveolar hissing sibilants [s] and [z], the back of the tongue forms a narrow channel (is grooved) to focus the stream of air more intensely, resulting in a high pitch. With the hushing sibilants (occasionally termed shibilants), such as English [ʃ], [tʃ], [ʒ], and [dʒ], the tongue is flatter, and the resulting pitch lower.[2][3]
A broader category is stridents, which include more fricatives than sibilants such as uvulars. Because all sibilants are also stridents, the terms are sometimes used interchangeably. However, the terms do not mean the same thing. The English stridents are /f, v, s, z, ʃ, ʒ, tʃ, dʒ/. Sibilants are a higher pitched subset of the stridents. The English sibilants are /s, z, ʃ, ʒ, tʃ, dʒ/. On the other hand, /f/ and /v/ are stridents, but not sibilants, because they are lower in pitch.[4][5][6]
"Stridency" refers to the perceptual intensity of the sound of a sibilant consonant, or obstacle fricatives or affricates, which refers to the critical role of the teeth in producing the sound as an obstacle to the airstream. Non-sibilant fricatives and affricates produce their characteristic sound directly with the tongue or lips etc. and the place of contact in the mouth, without secondary involvement of the teeth.[citation needed]
The characteristic intensity of sibilants means that small variations in tongue shape and position are perceivable, with the result that there are many sibilant types that contrast in various languages."

This overview traces the primary mechanisms of tonogenesis (tone‑creation), the major areal developments in Southeast Asia, Africa, and the Americas, and the subsequent evolution of complex tone systems.
**What is tonogenesis?**
Tonogenesis is the process by which distinctive tonal contrasts develop from earlier non‑tonal features, most commonly through the reanalysis of laryngeal or voicing distinctions on consonants as pitch differences on adjacent vowels ([Wikipedia][1]).
---
## 1. Phonetic sources of tone
1. **Laryngeal and voicing contrasts**
* In many languages, the loss or weakening of voicing distinctions on consonants (e.g. between *b* vs. *p*) leaves residual differences in pitch onset on the following vowel, which over time can become phonologized as tone.
* Voiced consonants often induce a lower voice—or breathy voice—on the following vowel, while voiceless aspirated consonants induce a higher pitch ([Frontiers][2], [ResearchGate][3]).
2. **Final consonant loss**
* In Chinese, the four tones of Middle Chinese (平 píng, 上 shǎng, 去 qù, 入 rù) are each reflexes of different lost final consonants in Old Chinese. For instance, words that ended in *-ʔ* developed a rising tone, while those ending in *-s* developed a departing tone ([Wikipedia][4]).
3. **Vowel quality and duration**
* Although less common, differences in vowel length or quality can also provide the phonetic groundwork for tone split or merger. These tend to play a secondary role compared to consonant‑induced effects ([haskinslabs.org][5]).
---
## 2. Major areal developments
### Southeast Asia
* **Sino‑Tibetan** (e.g. Chinese): Proto‑Sino‑Tibetan was likely non‑tonal; tone developed in Early Middle Chinese via final consonant loss and laryngeal splits, then spread areally to neighboring Tibeto‑Burman and Vietic languages ([PubMed][6], [Wikipedia][4]).
* **Austroasiatic** (e.g. Vietnamese): Vietnamese tonogenesis mirrors Chinese patterns—contrastive voicing on initials conditioned pitch on vowels, and later the loss of final glottal stops and *-s* created additional contour tones ([Oxford Research Encyclopedias][7], [Reddit][8]).
