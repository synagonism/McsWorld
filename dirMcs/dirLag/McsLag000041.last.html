"Turkic languages are famous for their rich vowel systems and systematic vowel-harmony. While individual languages differ slightly, most modern Turkic languages inherited an eight-vowel inventory from Proto-Turkic:
| Feature | Front | Back |
| Unrounded high | **i** /i/ | **ı** /ɯ/ |
| Rounded high | **ü** /y/ | **u** /u/ |

---
### Proto-Turkic
Reconstructed vowels (≈ 8):
`*i, *ï, *ü, *u, *e, *ä, *ö, *o`
(“\*ä” corresponds to low front /a/ in many modern varieties.)
---
### Examples in Key Modern Turkic Languages
| Language | Inventory |
| **Turkish** | /i, e, ɯ, a, y, ø, u, o/ |
| **Azerbaijani** | /i, e, ə, a, y, ø, u, o, ɯ/ (Latin: i, e, ə, a, ü, ö, u, o, ı) |
| **Kazakh** | /i, ɪ, e, æ, ɯ, u, o, ø, y/ (often transcribed ä for /æ/) |
| **Kyrgyz** | /i, ɪ, e, æ, ɯ, u, o, œ, y/ (very similar to Kazakh, with minor dialectal tweaks) |
| **Uzbek** | /i, e, a, o, u/ (has merged the two front-rounded vowels into /e/ or /i/, yielding a six-vowel system) |
| **Uyghur** | /i, e, ɑ, o, u, œ, y/ (front vs. back and rounded vs. unrounded distinctions largely intact) |
| **Turkmen** | /i, e, a, o, u, ä, ö, ü/ (retains eight-vowel harmony very close to the Proto-Turkic pattern) |
---
### Vowel Harmony
Most Turkic languages enforce **two-dimensional** harmony:
1. **Back vs. Front**
– vowels in a word are all from the back set {a, ı, o, u} or all from the front set {e, i, ö, ü}
2. **Roundedness** (in languages with mid-vowel rounding distinctions)
– high vowels also harmonize for rounded vs. unrounded within their height class
E.g., in Turkish **“kapı”** \[kaˈpɯ] “door” (back + unrounded), but **“gülüm”** \[gyˈlym] “my rose” (front + rounded).
---
### Notes on Variation
* **Azerbaijani** adds a low central vowel /ə/ (schwa).
* **Uzbek** lost front-rounded /ü, ö/, merging them into front-unrounded vowels.
* **Kazakh** and **Kyrgyz** often show additional allophones and a marginal ninth vowel \[ɪ] (near-close front unrounded).
* **Uyghur** orthography (Uyghur Arabic) represents vowels slightly differently but phonology remains close to the eight-vowel pattern.
---
#### Summary
* **Proto-Turkic**: 8 vowels
* **Core pattern**: /i, e, ɯ, a, y, ø, u, o/
* **Harmony**: front/back × rounded/unrounded
* **Modern tweaks**: some languages add schwa (Azer.), drop rounded vowels (Uzbek), or cram in marginal near-high vowels (Kazakh/Kyrgyz)."

### A Few Notes on Adaptation
* **Perso-Arabic → Latin/Cyrillic** reforms almost always involved reducing the number of vowels in orthography to match Turkish/Azerbaijani’s eight-vowel system, plus diacritics (e.g. **ö, ü, ñ**).
* **Soviet-era scripts** sought phonemic transparency but often introduced new letters (e.g. Kazakh’s ‹ә, ғ, қ, ң, ө, ұ›).
* **21st-century Latinization** (e.g. Kazakh’s 2017–2025 plan) emphasizes keyboard-friendliness: digraphs replace diacritics (e.g. ‹sh› for ‹ш›, ‹ng› for ‹ң›).
* **Uyghur Arabic** remains in wide everyday use (home, signage, publishing), but Latin (“Uyghur Pinyin”) and Cyrillic are taught for internationalization and computing.

Most Turkic languages have cycled through:
1. **Orkhon “runiform”** → 2. **Uyghur/Sogdian-derived cursive** → 3. **Perso-Arabic** → 4. **Latin** (interrupted by) **Cyrillic** → 5. **Latin** (today)

* **\*bar-mïš** ("He apparently went / It is said that he went")

* **\*-r (Aorist/Indefinite):** For habitual actions, general truths, or future intent.
* **\*bar-ur-men** ("I go / I will go")
* **\*-y (Present Progressive):** For actions happening right now. It was originally a verb **\*er-** ("to be") that was suffixed.
* **\*bar-a-yïn** (< **\*bar-a + er-men**) ("I am going")
**Optative (Desire/Volition):**
* **\*-gAy:** Expresses a wish or intention.
* **\*bar-ɣay-men** ("I want to go / May I go")
**Imperative:**
* The bare stem was the imperative for the 2nd person singular.
* **\*bar!** ("Go!")
* Other persons had specific suffixes like **\*-(X)l** (2nd pl.) and **\*-(X)yXn** (1st pl.).
#### B. Non-Finite Forms (Verbal Nouns, Participles, Converbs)
These forms created subordinate clauses or were used as nouns/adjectives.
**Participles (Relative Clauses):**
* **\*-dXk (Past Participle):** "The one who did X"
* **\*bar-dïk** ("the one who went")
* **\*-mXš (Narrative Participle):** "The one who (apparently) did X"
* **\*bar-mïš** ("the one who went (it is said)")
* **\*-r (Aorist Participle):** "The one who does/will do X"
* **\*bar-ur** ("the one who goes")
**Verbal Nouns (Infinitive):**
* **\*-mA-k:** The dictionary form or abstract action of the verb.
* **\*bar-ma-k** ("the act of going")
**Converbs (Adverbial Clauses):**
* **\*-p (Converb):** "and then..." or "by doing..."
* **\*bar-ïp** ("and (he) went...")
* **\*-A (Converb):** "while..." or "upon..."
* **\*bar-a** ("while going...")
### 6. Personal Endings
These marked the subject of the verb. There were two main sets: one for **predicative** forms (like the aorist in **\*-r**) and one for **possessive/oblique** forms (like the past tense in **\*-dX**).
| Person | Predicative (e.g., Aorist) | Possessive (e.g., Past Tense) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1st Sing.** | **\*-mXn** | **\*-m** |
| **2nd Sing.** | **\*-sXn** | **\*-ŋ** |
| **3rd Sing.** | **— (zero)** | **— (zero)** |
| **1st Pl.** | **\*-mXz** | **\*-k** |
| **2nd Pl.** | **\*-sXz** | **\*-ŋVz** |
| **3rd Pl.** | **\*-lAr** | **\*-lAr** |
**Examples:**
* **Aorist:** **\*bar-ur-men** ("I go")
* **Definite Past:** **\*bar-dï-m** ("I went")
### Summary Example: "To be fed"
Let's build a complex form: "They were not fed."
1. **Stem:** **\*yẹ-** ("to eat")
2. **Causative Voice:** **\*yẹ-t-** ("to feed")
3. **Passive Voice:** **\*yẹ-t-il-** ("to be fed")
4. **Negation:** **\*yẹ-t-il-me-** ("to not be fed")
5. **Past Tense:** **\*yẹ-t-il-me-dï-** ("was not fed")
6. **3rd Person Plural:** **\*yẹ-t-il-me-dï-lAr** ("They were not fed.")
This intricate structure is the foundation from which all Turkic languages, from Turkish and Azerbaijani to Uzbek and Yakut, developed their own verb systems."
