## 1. From Proto‑Indo‑European to Proto‑Slavic
* **≈ 4500 – 2500 BC: Proto‑Indo‑European (PIE)**
The ancestor of most European and many South Asian languages. PIE speakers spread across the steppes and into Europe.
* **≈ 2500 – 1500 BC: Satemization**
A sound shift in eastern Indo‑European that turned PIE palatovelars into sibilants. This “satem” innovation links the future Balto‑Slavic family with Indo‑Iranian.
* **≈ 1500 – 500 BC: Proto‑Balto‑Slavic**
Baltic and Slavic remained a close-knit subgroup. Shared changes include the **RUKI rule** (s > š after *r, u, k, i*), liquid metathesis, and early laryngeal effects.
* **≈ 500 BC – 0 AD: Divergence of Baltic vs. Slavic**
Baltic held onto more archaic inflections, while the Slavic side underwent the **first and second palatalizations** (changes of k, g, x before front vowels) and reduction of certain vowels.
---
## 2. Common Slavic (≈ 0 – 800 AD)
This was the unity period before the dialectal break. Key innovations:
1. **Jers (Reduced Vowels)**
Two ultra‑short vowels (*ъ, ь*) developed. Their later “strong” vs. “weak” reflexes drove much of Slavic’s morphological reshaping.
2. **Yat (*ě*) and Pleophony**
Distinct vowel *ě* led over time to varied reflexes (e.g. Russian e vs. Bulgarian я) and new vowel‑consonant sequences like TorT → ToraT.
3. **Stress Mobility**
Proto‑Slavic preserved a mobile pitch accent, later fixed in some languages and lost in others.

Several key sound changes unite Baltic and Slavic, distinguishing them from other Indo‑European branches:
* **Palatalization of velars**: Proto-IE *k, g, gh* became palatal or affricate in certain environments (e.g. Proto-IE \*\*ḱ > PSb *ś*).

* **Consonants**: Rich system including voiced/unvoiced stops (p b, t d, k ɡ), affricates (c dz, č dž), fricatives (s z, š ž, x), nasals (m n), liquids (l, r), and palatalized (“soft”) counterparts.
