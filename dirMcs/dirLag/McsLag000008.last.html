diacritic-mark of letter

description::
"A diacritic mark is a symbol added to a letter that alters its pronunciation or distinguishes it in some way from a similar letter. These marks are common in many languages and serve various purposes, such as indicating accent, tone, or pronunciation.
Here are some common diacritic marks and their uses:
1. **Acute Accent (´)**: Used in languages like Spanish (é), French (é), and Portuguese (é) to indicate a particular vowel sound or stress.
2. **Grave Accent (`)**: Found in French (è), Italian (è), and Greek (ὲ), it often indicates a different vowel sound or stress.
3. **Circumflex (ˆ)**: Used in French (ê), Portuguese (ê), and Romanian (â), it often signifies a change in vowel quality.
4. **Tilde (˜)**: Common in Spanish (ñ) and Portuguese (ã), it indicates nasalization of the vowel or consonant.
5. **Umlaut or Diaeresis (¨)**: Seen in German (ö, ü) and French (ë, ï), it alters the vowel sound.
6. **Cedilla (¸)**: Used in French (ç) and Turkish (ç), it changes the pronunciation of the letter 'c' from a hard to a soft sound.
7. **Macron (¯)**: Found in Latvian (ā), Hawaiian (ā), and some transliterations, it indicates a long vowel sound.
8. **Breve (˘)**: Seen in Romanian (ă) and Turkish (ğ), it can indicate a short vowel sound or a specific pronunciation.
9. **Ring (˚)**: Used in Scandinavian languages like Swedish (å) and Danish (å), it changes the vowel sound.
10. **Dot (˙)**: Found in languages like Turkish (ı) and Lithuanian (ė), it often changes the pronunciation or differentiates between letters.
Diacritic marks are essential for correct pronunciation and meaning in many languages, and their usage varies widely across different linguistic systems."
