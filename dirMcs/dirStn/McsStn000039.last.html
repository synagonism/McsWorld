# Albania
## Geography
(https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Valbona_nga_Kukaj.jpg) *Albania’s landscape is dominated by rugged mountains and river valleys, as seen here in the Albanian Alps.* Albania is located in southeastern Europe on the Balkan Peninsula, bordered by Montenegro to the northwest, Kosovo to the northeast, North Macedonia to the east, and Greece to the south (https://www.britannica.com/place/Albania/Land). To the west, its coastline spans about 476 km along the Adriatic and Ionian Seas, with Italy lying roughly 80 km across the Adriatic (https://www.britannica.com/place/Albania/Land). About three-quarters of Albania’s territory is mountainous or hilly (over 200 m elevation) (https://www.britannica.com/place/Albania/Land), including ranges like the Albanian Alps in the north. The highest peak is Mount Korab at approximately 2,764 m (https://www.britannica.com/place/Albania/Land), while lowland plains are mostly along the narrow coastal strip. Albania also has significant lakes and rivers: the Drin is the longest river, and lakes such as Lake Shkodër in the northwest and the Ohrid-Prespa lake system in the southeast are among the oldest in Europe (https://www.britannica.com/place/Albania/Land) (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography_of_Albania).
Albania’s climate varies from a **Mediterranean** climate on the coast to a **continental** climate inland. Coastal areas have hot, dry summers and mild, wet winters, with average winter temperatures around 7°C and summer highs around 30–32°C (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography_of_Albania). In the interior and highlands, winters are colder and snowy – the northern mountains experience subarctic conditions in winter – while summers are warm but cooler than the coast (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography_of_Albania). This range of microclimates, influenced by altitude and latitude, contributes to rich biodiversity. Natural features include sandy and pebble beaches along the Albanian Riviera, fertile valleys, and karst limestone formations. The country’s largest lake, Lake Shkodër, and the deep tectonic Lake Ohrid (a UNESCO heritage site) are ecologically important, and Albania’s varied terrain supports diverse flora and fauna ((https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography_of_Albania).
## History
**Ancient and Ottoman Era:** The region of present-day Albania has been inhabited since antiquity by Illyrian tribes and was influenced by Greek colonies on the coast (such as Apollonia and Durrës) (https://www.britannica.com/topic/history-of-Albania). Illyrian kingdoms resisted Roman expansion but were eventually defeated – by 168 BCE the Romans had conquered Illyria and incorporated it into the empire (https://www.britannica.com/topic/history-of-Albania). After the Roman Empire’s division in 395 CE, the area fell under the Byzantine (Eastern Roman) Empire. In the **Middle Ages**, local feudal states emerged, but by the late 15th century the Ottoman Turks had conquered the territory. Albanian national hero **Gjergj Kastrioti “Skanderbeg”** led a resistance that halted Ottoman advances for decades, but after his death the Ottomans fully subdued Albania (the last stronghold, Shkodër, fell in 1479) (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Albania). Albania remained under Ottoman rule for over four centuries, during which time many Albanians adopted Islam and the region experienced Islamic and Ottoman cultural influences.
**Independence and 20th Century:** Albania declared its independence from the Ottoman Empire on 28 November 1912, amid the Balkan Wars (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Albania). The Great Powers recognized a smaller Albanian state in 1913. The early years of independence were tumultuous: during **World War I**, Albania’s territory was occupied by various armies and the country had no stable central government. After the war, Albania was restored as a sovereign state, and in 1925 it became a republic. Ahmed Zogu, a former prime minister, declared himself King Zog I in 1928, establishing a short-lived monarchy (https://after-dictatorship.org/en/countries/europe/albania/the-country.html). In 1939, on the eve of World War II, Albania was invaded by Fascist Italy; the Italians were later replaced by Nazi German occupation in 1943. Albanian partisan fighters (including communist-led resistance) fought the occupiers, and by late 1944 the partisans liberated the country and took power (https://after-dictatorship.org/en/countries/europe/albania/the-country.html).
**Communist Period:** After WWII, Albania became a communist state led by Enver Hoxha, who ruled from 1944 until his death in 1985. Hoxha established a Stalinist dictatorship, eliminating political opposition and isolating Albania from the outside world (https://after-dictatorship.org/en/countries/europe/albania/the-country.html). The communist regime broke ties even with its former allies – first Yugoslavia, then the Soviet Union in 1961, and finally China by 1978 – pursuing a path of autarky and extreme isolation (https://after-dictatorship.org/en/countries/europe/albania/the-country.html). During this period, religion was banned (in 1967 Albania declared itself the world’s first atheist state), and the regime built tens of thousands of concrete bunkers across the country fearing invasion. By the late 1980s, Albania was one of Europe’s most economically backward and isolated countries.
**Modern Era:** The communist government began to falter as democratic revolutions swept Eastern Europe. In 1990–1991, protests and a mass exodus forced the regime to allow multiparty elections (https://after-dictatorship.org/en/countries/europe/albania/the-country.html). The first elections in March 1991 still saw the communists (rebranded as socialists) win, but by 1992 a non-communist government took office under President Sali Berisha of the Democratic Party (https://after-dictatorship.org/en/countries/europe/albania/the-country.html). The 1990s were chaotic – a nationwide collapse of pyramid investment schemes in 1997 led to unrest and near civil war, requiring international intervention to restore order (https://after-dictatorship.org/en/countries/europe/albania/the-country.html). After 1997, Albania stabilized and made progress in rebuilding its institutions. The country joined NATO in 2009 and has sought integration with Europe. In 2014 Albania was granted EU candidate status, and in July 2022 it formally opened accession negotiations with the European Union (https://www.consilium.europa.eu/en/policies/albania/). Today, Albania is a parliamentary democracy, and despite remaining challenges with corruption and development, it has a growing economy and improving ties with its Balkan neighbors and the West.
## Culture
Albania’s culture is a blend of its ancient heritage and more recent influences. The **Albanian language** (Shqip) is the official language and is unique as an independent branch of the Indo-European family. It has two main dialects – **Gheg** in the north and **Tosk** in the south – with the Shkumbin River roughly dividing the dialect zones (https://www.worldatlas.com/articles/important-aspects-of-the-culture-of-albania.html). The standard written language is based on Tosk. Albanians take great pride in their language, which is believed to descend from ancient Illyrian (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture_of_Albania). In terms of religion, Albania is a secular state with a legacy of religious diversity. The majority of the population has historically been Muslim (due to Ottoman influence), alongside significant Catholic and Orthodox Christian communities (https://www.worldatlas.com/articles/important-aspects-of-the-culture-of-albania.html). However, religiosity is generally low, and Albania is known for its **religious tolerance** – Muslim and Christian Albanians have coexisted peacefully, and families often celebrate each other’s holidays. Traditional social codes like the **Kanun** emphasize values of honor and hospitality. The Kanun’s honor code of **“Besa”** obliges Albanians to keep promises and offer protection to guests, a principle that famously led many Albanian families to shelter refugees (including Jews during WWII) regardless of religion (https://www.worldatlas.com/articles/important-aspects-of-the-culture-of-albania.html). Respect for guests and elders, and strong family loyalty, remain important cultural norms.
(https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Sofra_Dardane_-_Bajram_Curr_01.JPG) *Traditional Albanian folk dancers in national costume perform at the “Sofra Dardane” festival.* Albanian culture is rich in folklore, music, and dance. **Folk music** is a core part of national identity, with distinct styles in the north and south (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture_of_Albania). In the northern highlands, epic songs accompanied by instruments like the lahuta (lute) are common, while southern Albania is famous for its polyphonic singing. In fact, Albanian **iso-polyphonic** singing from the south is recognized by UNESCO as an Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture_of_Albania). Folklore festivals and ensembles keep these musical traditions alive, and traditional dances with colorful costumes (as pictured) are performed during national holidays and weddings. Modern Albanian music and literature have also flourished, producing internationally known artists and writers.
Albanian **cuisine** reflects Mediterranean flavors with local twists. Olive oil is a staple and has been used in cooking since antiquity (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture_of_Albania). Meals often feature a variety of fresh vegetables (tomatoes, peppers, eggplant, etc.), herbs like oregano and mint, and meats such as lamb, beef, or chicken (https://www.worldatlas.com/articles/important-aspects-of-the-culture-of-albania.html). Along the coast, seafood is abundant and popular. Common dishes include *byrek* (a flaky pastry pie filled with spinach, cheese, or meat) and *tavë kosi* (baked lamb and yogurt). Grilled meats, stews, and fresh salads are everyday fare. For dessert, sweets like *baklava* (a syrupy walnut pastry) reflect Ottoman influence. Eating together is central to Albanian hospitality – guests are generously welcomed with coffee, *raki* (fruit brandy), and homemade food. This warmth toward guests is a point of pride, tied to the concept of **hospitality** in the Kanun (https://www.worldatlas.com/articles/important-aspects-of-the-culture-of-albania.html). Overall, Albania’s cultural heritage – from its language and traditional costumes to its music, dance, and cuisine – is a source of national pride that the society works to preserve even as it modernizes.
## Economy
Albania has a developing **market economy** that has grown steadily since the 1990s, though it remains one of Europe’s smaller economies. The GDP was around $23.5 billion in 2023 and GDP per capita about $8,500 (https://www.focus-economics.com/countries/albania/). The economy has transitioned from a communist centrally-planned system to a largely open, service-oriented economy. Today, the **services sector** (including trade, tourism, and telecommunications) makes up over half of GDP, followed by **industry** (manufacturing, mining, energy) at about one-quarter and **agriculture** around one-fifth (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Albania). Key industries include **agriculture** (especially fruits, vegetables, and livestock), **food processing**, **textiles and footwear** manufacturing, **oil and mineral extraction** (Albania has reserves of oil, natural gas, and chromium), and **hydropower energy** (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Albania). In recent years, **tourism** has become a major contributor to growth, especially during summer, injecting billions in revenue (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Albania). The country’s real GDP growth has averaged around 3–4% annually in the past decade, and even in 2023 amid global challenges Albania posted about 3.4% growth (https://www.worldbank.org/en/country/albania/overview).
Income from abroad is significant – **remittances** sent by Albanian diaspora workers (notably from Italy and Greece) have long bolstered the economy, accounting for an estimated 15% of GDP in the 2000s (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Albania) (and still around 9% of GDP in 2022 (https://globaledge.msu.edu/countries/albania/memo). These funds help many households and fuel consumer spending. Unemployment has improved but remains relatively high, and a large informal economy persists. Albania faces important **challenges** in its economic development: infrastructure needs modernization, and the business climate is hampered by bureaucracy and corruption. The country struggles with **endemic corruption** and needs to strengthen the rule of law and governance to attract more foreign investment (https://www.focus-economics.com/countries/albania/). Efforts are underway to improve tax collection, reform the judiciary, and invest in education and job training. Albania’s strategic goals include diversifying its economy, developing its energy and tourism potential, and eventually joining the EU common market. Continued structural reforms – and success in reducing corruption and improving transparency – are seen as critical for sustaining growth and ensuring long-term prosperity (https://www.focus-economics.com/countries/albania/).

The country was long off the typical tourist radar, but it is gaining fame for its unspoiled beaches, dramatic mountains, historical sites, and low prices compared to other European destinations (https://www.focus-economics.com/countries/albania/). Visitor numbers have been growing every year: in 2019 Albania welcomed about 6.4 million foreign tourists (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_of_Albania), and by 2023 it hit a record of over **10 million** international tourist arrivals (https://www.visit-tirana.com/news/10-1-million-tourists-visited-albania-in-2023/). This boom is transforming formerly quiet coastal villages and bringing investment in hotels and infrastructure. Travelers often remark on the friendly reception – Albania is considered safe and very welcoming to tourists, with low levels of crime targeting visitors (https://theculturetrip.com/europe/albania/articles/11-things-to-know-before-travelling-to-albania). English and Italian are spoken in many tourist areas, especially by younger Albanians, making it easier to get around.

Berat features white hillside houses with distinctive windows and a hilltop castle (https://www.leisure.com/vacations/albania-vacation), while Gjirokastër boasts stone-roofed houses and a large fortress overlooking the town (https://www.leisure.com/vacations/albania-vacation). Whenever you go, Albania’s mix of natural beauty, culture, and affordability makes it an increasingly popular and rewarding destination.
## Politics and Governance
Albania is a **unitary parliamentary republic** governed under a constitution adopted in 1998 (https://globaledge.msu.edu/countries/albania/government). Its political system has three branches: executive, legislative, and judiciary. The **President of the Republic** is the head of state with largely ceremonial powers. The president is elected by the Parliament (not by popular vote) for a 5-year term and may serve up to two terms. The current president is **Bajram Begaj**, who was elected by Parliament in July 2022 (https://eurydice.eacea.ec.europa.eu/national-education-systems/albania/main-executive-and-legislative-bodies). The **head of government** is the Prime Minister, who holds executive power and leads the Council of Ministers (cabinet) (https://www.britannica.com/place/Albania/Constitutional-framework). The prime minister is typically the leader of the majority party or coalition in the unicameral Parliament (known as the **Kuvendi**), which has 140 members elected for four-year terms.
Since the end of communist rule, Albania has developed a multi-party democracy. Power has alternated primarily between two major political parties: the center-right **Democratic Party** and the center-left **Socialist Party** (heir to the former communist party). The **Socialist Party** has been in government since 2013, led by Prime Minister **Edi Rama**, who first took office in 2013 and has won re-election multiple times (https://after-dictatorship.org/en/countries/europe/albania/the-country.html). Under Rama’s leadership, the government has pursued policies aimed at modernizing infrastructure, improving tourism, and advancing Albania’s EU integration. The opposition, currently led by the Democratic Party, has at times organized protests accusing the government of corruption or authoritarian tendencies, reflecting the intense partisanship of Albanian politics. Governance in Albania has notably improved over the decades, but **corruption** and weak rule of law remain issues; in recent years the country undertook a thorough vetting of judges and prosecutors to root out corruption in the judiciary, as part of reforms demanded by the EU.
On the international front, Albania has oriented itself firmly toward the West. It joined the NATO alliance in 2009 (https://after-dictatorship.org/en/countries/europe/albania/the-country.html) and contributes to regional security initiatives. Closer integration with Europe is a top strategic goal: Albania was granted EU candidate status in 2014 and, after implementing required reforms, **accession negotiations** with the European Union officially began in July 2022 (https://www.consilium.europa.eu/en/policies/albania/). The EU accession process is driving many political and legal reforms, particularly in fighting organized crime and strengthening public institutions. In terms of administrative structure, Albania is divided into 12 counties and 61 municipalities after recent territorial reforms, aiming to decentralize governance. The country’s foreign relations are generally positive – it maintains good ties with neighboring Kosovo (with whom it shares ethnic and cultural links), NATO allies, and has improved relations with historic rival Serbia through regional dialogues. **Current affairs** in Albanian politics often center on anti-corruption efforts, economic development, and managing emigration (many Albanians seek work abroad). While challenges persist, Albania’s governance has become more stable and transparent over time, and its ongoing transformation is closely tied to its ambition to join the European Union and provide a better future for its citizens (https://www.focus-economics.com/countries/albania/)."

"Albanian adjectives agree with the noun they modify in **gender**, **number**, **definiteness** and (in full syntax) **case**. They also exhibit **comparative** and **superlative** forms. Below is a concise overview.
---
## 1. Placement
* **Attributive** (before/after noun):
Most adjectives follow the noun:
⭢ **shtëpi e bukur** “a beautiful house”
* **Predicative** (after linking verb):
⭢ **Shtëpia është e bukur.** “The house is beautiful.”
---
## 2. Definiteness
Every noun in Albanian can be **definite** or **indefinite**, and adjectives must match:
| | Indefinite | Definite |
| Masc. sg. | një libër i ri | libri i ri |
| Fem. sg. | një shtëpi e madhe | shtëpia e madhe |
| Plural | dy libra të rinj | librat e rinj |
* **Indefinite** adjective forms attach after the noun with a linking particle (i/e/të).
* **Definite** adjectives take a suffix and agree directly.
---
## 3. Agreement by Gender & Number
### 3.1 Singular
| Gender | Indefinite | Definite |
| Masculine | libër *i ri* | libri *i riu* |
| Feminine | shtëpi *e re* | shtëpia *e reja* |
### 3.2 Plural
| Gender | Indefinite | Definite |
| Mixed/ Masc | libra *të rinj* | librat *e rinjtë* |
| Feminine | shtëpi *të reja* | shtëpitë *e reja* |
⭢ **Note:** The linking particle for indefinite adjectives is **’i’** with masculine, **’e’** with feminine; for plural it becomes **’të’**.
---
## 4. Case Morphology
Albanian has five cases (Nominative, Genitive, Dative, Accusative, Vocative). In everyday speech, adjectives mainly show case distinctions in **formal** or **literary** registers. For a brief snapshot (masculine singular):
| Case | Indef. Adj. | Def. Adj. |
| Nominative | i mirë | i miri |
| Genitive | i mirë | i mirë |
| Dative | i mirë | i mritur |
| Accusative | i mirë | i mirë |
| Vocative | mirë! | miri! |
⭢ In practice, many forms collapse, especially in the non-definite.
---
## 5. Types of Adjectives
1. **Qualitative**: express quality/adjective degree
* *i madh* “big”, *i vogël* “small”
2. **Relational**: denote relation or origin
* *shkollor* “school-related”, *francez* “French”
3. **Possessive** (derived from pronouns)
* *im*, *yt*, *i saj* “mine, yours, hers”
---
## 6. Comparison
* **Comparative**: formed with **më** + adjective
⭢ *Alex është më i gjatë se Ben.* “Alex is taller than Ben.”
* **Superlative**: **më** + definite adjective + noun
⭢ *Ai është më i gjatë në klasë.* “He is the tallest in the class.”
Irregular comparatives:
* *i mirë* → *më i miri* “the best”
* *i madh* → *më i madhi* “the biggest”
---
## 7. Summary of Key Endings
| Form | Masc. sg. | Fem. sg. | Plural |
| **Indefinite** | – **i** | – **e** | – **të** |
| **Definite** | **–i/u** | **–ja/e** | **–të/të** |
⭢ E.g., *libër i ri* (indef. masc.), *libri i riu* (def. masc.)
---
This should give you the essential framework for how adjectives behave in Albanian. Let me know if you’d like more on cases, irregular forms, or usage!"

"The **Albanian language** (Shqip) has a long and unique history, evolving over millennia. It belongs to the **Indo-European** language family but forms its **own independent branch**, meaning it does not closely resemble any other modern Indo-European language. Here’s an overview of its evolution:
---
### **1. Prehistoric Origins (Before 1st Millennium BCE)**
- The origins of Albanian are debated, but it is believed to descend from **Illyrian**, **Dacian**, or **Thracian**—ancient languages spoken in the Balkans.
- Some scholars favor **Illyrian origins**, as Illyrians inhabited much of present-day Albania and surrounding areas.
- Others argue for a **Dacian or Thracian link**, especially due to some similarities in place names.
---
### **2. Proto-Albanian (1st–5th Century CE)**
- During the **Roman era**, Albanian began diverging from its ancestor language.
- Latin heavily influenced Albanian, introducing many loanwords. Today, around **60% of the Albanian vocabulary** has Latin origins.
- Examples of Latin borrowings in Albanian:
- **lat. "aqua" → alb. "ujë"** (water)
- **lat. "fenestra" → alb. "dritare"** (window)
---
### **3. Medieval Albanian (6th–15th Century)**
- With the Slavic migrations (6th-7th centuries), Albanian absorbed **some Slavic words**, especially in the north.
- The earliest written evidence of Albanian comes from the **1462 baptismal formula** recorded by **Pal Engjëlli**, an Albanian Catholic priest.
- The **Gheg-Tosk dialect split** emerged, with **Gheg** spoken in the north and **Tosk** in the south.
---
### **4. Early Modern Albanian (16th–18th Century)**
- The first books in Albanian appeared:
- **1555** – "Meshari" (The Missal) by **Gjon Buzuku** (first known book in Albanian).
- **1592** – First dictionary compiled by Frang Bardhi.
- The **Ottoman period** (15th–20th century) brought **Turkish, Persian, and Arabic loanwords**.
---
### **5. Modern Albanian (19th Century–Present)**
- **19th century**: The Albanian National Awakening (Rilindja Kombëtare) led to the first efforts at standardizing the language.
- **1908**: The **Congress of Manastir** established a unified **Albanian alphabet** based on the Latin script.
- **20th century**: In 1972, the **Congress of Orthography** in Tirana created a standardized literary Albanian based on the **Tosk dialect** (used officially in Albania, Kosovo, and Albanian-speaking regions).
---
### **Key Features of Albanian Evolution**
✅ **Independent Indo-European branch**
✅ **Heavily influenced by Latin** (due to Roman rule)
✅ **Influences from Slavic, Greek, Turkish, and Italian**
✅ **Two main dialects**: **Gheg (north) & Tosk (south)**
✅ **Standardized alphabet in 1908**
---
Today, **Albanian is spoken by around 7.5 million people**, primarily in **Albania, Kosovo, North Macedonia, Montenegro, Serbia, Greece, Italy (Arbëresh dialect), and the Albanian diaspora**.
