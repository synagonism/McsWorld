descriptionLong::
"An economy (from Greek οίκος – "household" and νέμoμαι – "manage") is an area of the production, distribution and trade, as well as consumption of goods and services by different agents. Understood in its broadest sense, 'The economy is defined as a social domain that emphasize the practices, discourses, and material expressions associated with the production, use, and management of resources'.[1] A given economy is the result of a set of processes that involves its culture, values, education, technological evolution, history, social organization, political structure and legal systems, as well as its geography, natural resource endowment, and ecology, as main factors. These factors give context, content, and set the conditions and parameters in which an economy functions. In other words, the economic domain is a social domain of human practices and transactions. It does not stand alone.
Economic agents can be individuals, businesses, organizations, or governments. Economic transactions occur when two groups or parties agree to the value or price of the transacted good or service, commonly expressed in a certain currency. However, monetary transactions only account for a small part of the economic domain.
Economic activity is spurred by production which uses natural resources, labor and capital. It has changed over time due to technology (automation, accelerator of process, reduction of cost functions), innovation (new products, services, processes, expanding markets, diversification of markets, niche markets, increases revenue functions) such as, that which produces intellectual property and changes in industrial relations (most notably child labor being replaced in some parts of the world with universal access to education).
A market-based economy is one where goods and services are produced and exchanged according to demand and supply between participants (economic agents) by barter or a medium of exchange with a credit or debit value accepted within the network, such as a unit of currency. A command-based economy is one where political agents directly control what is produced and how it is sold and distributed. A green economy is low-carbon, resource efficient and socially inclusive. In a green economy, growth in income and employment is driven by public and private investments that reduce carbon emissions and pollution, enhance energy and resource efficiency, and prevent the loss of biodiversity and ecosystem services.[2] A gig economy is one in which short-term jobs are assigned or chosen via online platforms.[3] New economy is a term referred to the whole emerging ecosystem where new standards and practices were introduced, usually as a result of technological innovations."

">financial-system vs transacting-system:
The financial system and the transacting system are integral components of an economy, but they serve distinct purposes and functions.

1. **Financial System:**
- The financial system refers to the broader framework of institutions, markets, and intermediaries that facilitate the flow of funds and capital within an economy.
- It includes institutions such as banks, financial markets (stock markets, bond markets), insurance companies, and regulatory bodies.
- The financial system plays a crucial role in mobilizing savings, allocating capital, facilitating investments, and providing various financial services.

2. **Transacting System:**
- The transacting system, on the other hand, is more focused on the day-to-day transactions and exchange of goods and services within the economy.
- It involves the mechanisms through which individuals and businesses conduct transactions, such as buying and selling goods and services.
- Payment systems, currency, and methods of transferring value are essential components of the transacting system.

**Key Differences:**
1. **Scope and Function:**
- The financial system has a broader scope, encompassing institutions and mechanisms that deal with savings, investments, capital allocation, and risk management.
- The transacting system is more specific, dealing with the immediate exchange of economic value through transactions.

2. **Components:**
- The financial system includes institutions like banks and financial markets that facilitate the movement of money and capital.
- The transacting system involves the mechanisms and instruments used for everyday transactions, such as cash, checks, credit cards, and electronic payment systems.

3. **Long-Term vs. Short-Term:**
- The financial system is often associated with long-term financial activities, such as investment and capital formation.
- The transacting system is more focused on short-term, day-to-day economic transactions.

4. **Regulation:**
- The financial system is subject to extensive regulation to ensure stability, integrity, and fair practices in financial markets.
- The transacting system may also be subject to regulations, particularly in terms of payment systems and consumer protection, but the scope is generally narrower.

5. **Purpose:**
- The financial system's primary purpose is to facilitate the efficient allocation of resources, promote economic growth, and manage financial risks.
- The transacting system's primary purpose is to enable the exchange of goods and services, supporting the functioning of the economy on a daily basis.

In summary, while the financial system focuses on the broader aspects of financial activities, investment, and capital allocation, the transacting system deals with the immediate exchange of economic value in day-to-day transactions. Both systems are interconnected and essential for the overall functioning of an economy."

">financial law:
"Financial law" is a broad term that encompasses a range of legal principles, regulations, and statutes that govern financial transactions and activities. It is a specialized field of law that addresses the legal aspects of financial markets, institutions, and instruments. Key areas within financial law include:

1. **Banking Law:** Governs the activities of banks and financial institutions, including regulations related to lending, deposits, and other banking operations.

2. **Securities Law:** Regulates the issuance and trading of securities such as stocks and bonds. It aims to ensure transparency and protect investors from fraud and market manipulation.

3. **Investment Law:** Focuses on the regulation of investment activities, including rules for investment funds, investment advisers, and the protection of investors.

4. **Insurance Law:** Deals with the legal aspects of insurance contracts, the operation of insurance companies, and the rights and obligations of policyholders.

5. **Corporate Finance Law:** Involves regulations related to corporate fundraising, mergers and acquisitions, and other financial transactions undertaken by corporations.

6. **Consumer Finance Law:** Addresses the legal framework for consumer lending, including regulations to protect consumers from unfair or deceptive practices.

7. **Tax Law:** Encompasses regulations related to the taxation of individuals, businesses, and financial transactions. Tax law is closely linked to financial law, as it influences the structuring of financial transactions.

8. **International Finance Law:** Deals with legal issues arising in the context of cross-border financial transactions and activities, including international banking and finance regulations.

9. **Financial Regulation:** Encompasses a broad set of rules and regulations aimed at maintaining the stability and integrity of financial markets. Regulatory bodies, such as central banks and financial regulatory agencies, play a crucial role in overseeing financial institutions and ensuring compliance with the law.

10. **Anti-Money Laundering (AML) and Know Your Customer (KYC) Regulations:** Focus on preventing illegal financial activities such as money laundering and terrorist financing by imposing obligations on financial institutions to verify and monitor their customers.

Given the complexity and constant evolution of financial markets, financial law is dynamic and subject to ongoing changes and updates. Legal professionals specializing in financial law often work closely with financial institutions, regulatory bodies, and other stakeholders to navigate and ensure compliance with these intricate regulations."

">evolution of financial-law:
The evolution of financial law has been influenced by various factors, including changes in financial markets, advancements in technology, globalization, and the need for regulatory responses to financial crises. Here is an overview of the key stages in the evolution of financial law:

1. **Early Regulatory Frameworks:**
- **19th Century:** The development of modern financial law can be traced back to the 19th century when industrialization and the growth of capital markets led to the establishment of early regulatory frameworks. Banking laws and regulations started to emerge to address issues related to bank solvency and customer protection.

2. **Great Depression and Securities Regulation:**
- **1920s-1930s:** The stock market crash of 1929 and the subsequent Great Depression prompted the need for more comprehensive securities regulation. In the United States, the Securities Act of 1933 and the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 were enacted to regulate the issuance and trading of securities and to restore confidence in financial markets.

3. **Bretton Woods System:**
- **1944:** The Bretton Woods Agreement established a new international monetary system. The International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank were created to promote stability in exchange rates and international trade. This era marked increased international cooperation in financial matters.

4. **Deregulation and Innovation:**
- **Late 20th Century:** In the latter half of the 20th century, there was a trend toward financial deregulation in some countries, aiming to foster innovation and competition. This period saw the emergence of new financial instruments and the growth of derivatives markets.

5. **Financial Crises and Regulatory Reforms:**
- **Late 20th Century to Early 21st Century:** Financial crises, such as the Savings and Loan Crisis in the 1980s and the global financial crisis of 2008, prompted a reevaluation of regulatory frameworks. In response, regulatory reforms were introduced, including the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act in the United States.

6. **Technology and Fintech Regulation:**
- **21st Century:** The rise of financial technology (fintech) has introduced new challenges and opportunities. Regulators have had to adapt to the digitalization of financial services, addressing issues like cybersecurity, data protection, and the regulation of cryptocurrencies and blockchain technology.

7. **Globalization and International Cooperation:**
- **21st Century:** With the increasing interconnectedness of financial markets, there has been a growing emphasis on international cooperation in financial regulation. Organizations such as the Financial Stability Board (FSB) play a role in coordinating global regulatory efforts to ensure financial stability.

8. **Post-COVID-19 Challenges:**
- **2020s:** The COVID-19 pandemic has presented new challenges for financial law and regulation. Governments and regulators have implemented measures to stabilize economies, and issues such as business continuity, market volatility, and the impact on financial institutions have come to the forefront.

9. **Sustainable Finance and ESG:**
- **Current Trends:** There is a growing focus on integrating environmental, social, and governance (ESG) factors into financial decision-making. Sustainable finance and responsible investing are gaining prominence, leading to discussions about how financial law can support these initiatives.

The evolution of financial law is an ongoing process, shaped by the dynamic nature of financial markets, economic developments, technological advancements, and regulatory responses to emerging challenges. It reflects the continuous effort to strike a balance between promoting financial innovation and ensuring the stability and integrity of financial systems."

Financial law, the legal framework governing financial transactions and institutions, has evolved significantly throughout history, adapting to changing economic landscapes, technological advancements, and social norms. Its historical trajectory can be broadly divided into four phases:

**1. Early Development (Pre-1900)**
In its early stages, financial law emerged from customary practices and common law principles, primarily focused on safeguarding merchants and facilitating trade. The Lex mercatoria, a set of merchant customs developed in Europe, played a significant role in establishing norms for commercial transactions. Contract law, bankruptcy law, and securities law were the primary areas of focus during this period.

**2. Regulatory Expansion (1900-1970)**
The early 20th century witnessed a surge in financial innovation and economic growth, leading to increased government involvement in regulating the financial sector. The rise of central banks, the establishment of stock exchanges, and the introduction of new financial instruments spurred the need for a more comprehensive regulatory framework. Antitrust laws, banking laws, and securities laws were among the key areas of regulatory development.

**3. Global Integration and Market Reforms (1970s-2000)**
The post-war era saw a period of accelerated globalization and financial liberalization, characterized by the expansion of cross-border trade, financial markets, and the rise of multinational corporations. This period necessitated further expansion of financial law, with a focus on international cooperation, risk management, and consumer protection. The establishment of the Basel Accords, the development of derivative markets, and the introduction of consumer protection regulations are notable examples.

**4. Post-Financial Crisis Era: Resilience and Innovation (2008-Present)**
The global financial crisis of 2008 highlighted the need for stronger financial regulation and a more resilient financial system. The crisis prompted reforms aimed at enhancing risk management practices, strengthening capital requirements, and improving regulatory oversight. Additionally, technological advancements in fintech and blockchain have spurred the development of new financial products and services, requiring ongoing adaptation of financial law to address emerging regulatory challenges.

The evolution of financial law has been a dynamic and ongoing process, responding to the ever-changing complexities of the financial landscape. As financial markets continue to evolve, financial law will undoubtedly adapt to safeguard investors, protect consumers, and promote economic stability."

description::
"The System of National Accounts (SNA) is the internationally agreed standard set of recommendations on how to compile measures of economic activity. The SNA describes a coherent, consistent and integrated set of macroeconomic accounts in the context of a set of internationally agreed concepts, definitions, classifications and accounting rules.
In addition, the SNA provides an overview of economic processes, recording how production is distributed among consumers, businesses, government and foreign nations. It shows how income originating in production, modified by taxes and transfers, flows to these groups and how they allocate these flows to consumption, saving and investment. Consequently, the national accounts are one of the building blocks of macroeconomic statistics forming a basis for economic analysis and policy formulation.
The SNA is intended for use by all countries, having been designed to accommodate the needs of countries at different stages of economic development. It also provides an overarching framework for standards in other domains of economic statistics, facilitating the integration of these statistical systems to achieve consistency with the national accounts"
[{2020-11-07} https://unstats.un.org/unsd/nationalaccount/sna.asp]

"National Transfer Accounts: Understanding the Generational Economy
The goal of the National Transfer Accounts (NTA) project is to improve understanding of how population growth and changing population age structure influence economic growth, gender and generational equity, public finances, and other important features of the macro-economy. Research teams in more than 60 countries are constructing accounts that measure how people at each age produce, consume, and share resources, and save for the future. There are two main types of bankruptcy:
* **Chapter 7:** This is the most common type of bankruptcy, and it is used for individuals and businesses who cannot pay their debts and who have few assets to liquidate. In a Chapter 7 bankruptcy, a trustee is appointed to sell the debtor's nonexempt assets and distribute the proceeds to the creditors. The debtor's remaining debts are then discharged, which means that they are no longer legally obligated to repay them.
* **Chapter 13:** This type of bankruptcy is used for individuals who have regular income and who want to repay their debts over a period of three to five years. In a Chapter 13 bankruptcy, the debtor creates a repayment plan that outlines how they will pay their debts to their creditors. The court will approve the plan if it is fair and equitable to all creditors.


**Eligibility Requirements**
There are a number of eligibility requirements for filing for bankruptcy. These requirements vary depending on the type of bankruptcy filed, but some of the general requirements include:
* The debtor must be insolvent, which means that they must not be able to pay their debts as they become due.
* The debtor must have filed a tax return for the most recent tax year.
* The debtor must not have filed a bankruptcy petition in the past 180 days.
* The debtor must not have been convicted of a felony debt offense.

**The Bankruptcy Process**
The bankruptcy process typically involves the following steps:
* **Filing the petition:** The debtor files a bankruptcy petition with the bankruptcy court. The petition contains information about the debtor's assets, liabilities, and income.
* **Meeting of creditors:** The debtor must attend a meeting of creditors where they will answer questions about their finances.
* **Confirmation hearing:** The debtor and their creditors will appear before the bankruptcy judge at a confirmation hearing. The judge will approve or deny the debtor's repayment plan (in Chapter 13) or will discharge the debtor's debts (in Chapter 7).

econEco.circular-003

description::
"The circular economy is a way of building a world with no waste."
[{2020-11-13} https://www.weforum.org/agenda/2020/11/4-lessons-from-nature-to-build-a-circular-economy]
===
"A circular economy (often referred to simply as "circularity"[1]) is an economic system aimed at eliminating waste and the continual use of resources. Circular systems employ reuse, sharing, repair, refurbishment, remanufacturing and recycling to create a closed-loop system, minimising the use of resource inputs and the creation of waste, pollution and carbon emissions.[2] The circular economy aims to keep products, equipment and infrastructure[3] in use for longer, thus improving the productivity of these resources. All "waste" should become "food" for another process: either a by-product or recovered resource for another industrial process or as regenerative resources for nature (e.g., compost). This regenerative approach is in contrast to the traditional linear economy, which has a "take, make, dispose" model of production.[4]"
[{2020-11-13} https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_economy]
