description::
">sociology of technology:
The sociology of technology is an interdisciplinary field that examines the social and cultural aspects of technology. It explores how technology shapes society and how society shapes technology. The field draws on insights from a variety of disciplines, including sociology, anthropology, history, philosophy, and science and technology studies (STS).

**Key themes in the sociology of technology**
* **The social construction of technology**
Technology is not neutral; it is shaped by social values, norms, and interests. For example, the design of a car reflects the values of its creators and the society in which it is produced.
* **The social impacts of technology**
Technology can have a profound impact on society, both positive and negative. For example, the invention of the printing press led to a revolution in communication and the spread of knowledge, but it also contributed to the rise of mass media and the decline of traditional forms of storytelling.
* **The power and control of technology**
Technology can be used to empower or control people. For example, social media platforms can be used to connect people and share information, but they can also be used to spread misinformation and manipulate people's behavior.

**Examples of research in the sociology of technology**
* **The study of the digital divide**
The digital divide refers to the gap between people who have access to and can use technology and those who do not. Sociologists study the causes and consequences of the digital divide, and they develop strategies for bridging it.
* **The analysis of social media**
Sociologists study how people use social media and how these platforms shape social interactions and relationships. They also examine the impact of social media on social movements, political discourse, and public opinion.
* **The investigation of surveillance and privacy**
Sociologists study the implications of surveillance technologies for individual privacy and civil liberties. They also explore the social and cultural factors that shape the development and use of these technologies.
* **The examination of artificial intelligence and robotics**
Sociologists are investigating the potential social impacts of artificial intelligence and robotics, such as job displacement and the ethical implications of autonomous weapons.

**The sociology of technology is a growing field with a wide range of applications.**
* **Informing policy decisions**
Sociological research can be used to inform policy decisions about the development, use, and regulation of technology.
* **Understanding social change**
The sociology of technology can help us to understand how technology is changing society and how we can adapt to these changes.
* **Developing new technologies**
Sociological insights can be used to design new technologies that are more socially responsible and equitable."

description::
"In the context of spaceflight, a satellite is an object that has been intentionally placed into orbit. These objects are called artificial satellites to distinguish them from natural satellites such as Earth's Moon.
On 4 October 1957 the Soviet Union launched the world's first artificial satellite, Sputnik 1. Since then, about 8,900 satellites from more than 40 countries have been launched. According to a 2018 estimate, some 5,000 remain in orbit. Of those about 1,900 were operational, while the rest have lived out their useful lives and become space debris. Approximately 63% of operational satellites are in low Earth orbit, 6% are in medium-Earth orbit (at 20,000 km), 29% are in geostationary orbit (at 36,000 km) and the remaining 2% are in elliptic orbit. In terms of countries with the most satellites the USA significantly leads the way with 859 satellites, China is second with 250, and Russia third with 146. These are then followed by India (118), Japan (72) and the UK (52).[1] A few large space stations have been launched in parts and assembled in orbit. Over a dozen space probes have been placed into orbit around other bodies and become artificial satellites of the Moon, Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, a few asteroids,[2] a comet and the Sun.
Satellites are used for many purposes. Among several other applications, they can be used to make star maps and maps of planetary surfaces, and also take pictures of planets they are launched into. Common types include military and civilian Earth observation satellites, communications satellites, navigation satellites, weather satellites, and space telescopes. Space stations and human spacecraft in orbit are also satellites.
Satellites can operate by themselves or as part of a larger system, a satellite formation or satellite constellation.
Satellite orbits vary greatly, depending on the purpose of the satellite, and are classified in a number of ways. Well-known (overlapping) classes include low Earth orbit, polar orbit, and geostationary orbit.
A launch vehicle is a rocket that places a satellite into orbit. Usually, it lifts off from a launch pad on land. Some are launched at sea from a submarine or a mobile maritime platform, or aboard a plane (see air launch to orbit).
Satellites are usually semi-independent computer-controlled systems. Satellite subsystems attend many tasks, such as power generation, thermal control, telemetry, attitude control, scientific instrumentation, communication, etc."
